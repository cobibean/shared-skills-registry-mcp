# Website Copywriting Research Report

## Executive summary

Persuasive website copy is not word decoration. It is ethical, evidence-backed salesmanship that meets the reader at their current awareness level, channels an existing desire or anxiety, makes one credible promise, proves it, resolves objections, and asks for the smallest honest next action.

The skill’s source roles:

- **Cialdini**: persuasion mechanisms and ethical constraints.
- **Hopkins**: measurable salesmanship, specificity, research, samples, and testing.
- **Ogilvy**: big idea, one simple promise, reader respect, brand personality, and craft discipline.
- **Schwartz**: awareness levels, market sophistication, mass desire, mechanism, and page-length logic.

## What persuasive website copy does

Website copy moves a visitor through four states:

1. **Recognition:** “This is for me / this problem is mine.”
2. **Desire:** “I want the outcome this promises.”
3. **Belief:** “I believe this product can plausibly deliver.”
4. **Action:** “The next step is clear, safe, and worth it.”

Weak copy usually asks for action before recognition, desire, or belief exists.

## Old-school principles that still matter

### Advertising is salesmanship

Hopkins’s central claim is that advertising exists to sell and should justify itself by results. On websites, every page must have a job. A homepage is not a brochure; a pricing page is not a price table; a CTA is not a decorative button.

### The headline selects the reader

A website hero should hail the right visitor by problem, audience, outcome, or moment. It does not need to attract everyone.

### Specificity is credibility

Readers discount vague superlatives. Trade “powerful/easy/best” for concrete claims, numbers, mechanisms, examples, constraints, demos, and proof.

### One simple promise

Ogilvy’s strategy discipline says to reduce the strategy to one simple promise and deliver it. A hero with five promises has no main character.

### Awareness controls the opening

Schwartz’s awareness framework is the most useful strategic filter:

- **Most aware:** lead with offer/action.
- **Product aware:** lead with differentiation/proof.
- **Solution aware:** lead with mechanism/category fit.
- **Problem aware:** lead with precise problem and education.
- **Unaware:** lead with story, belief, identity, or surprising fact.

### Market sophistication controls believability

If the market has heard the same claim many times, direct benefit copy sounds naive. The copy must add specificity, a unique mechanism, stronger proof, or—when the market is exhausted—an honest identity/point-of-view frame.

### Persuasion must preserve trust

Use only naturally present persuasion: real proof, real authority, real scarcity, real reciprocity, real fit. Fake urgency and fake proof are not conversion tactics; they are trust debt.

## Conversion-copy framework

1. Surface: what page/component are we writing?
2. Conversion job: what should the visitor do next?
3. Audience: who is the right reader?
4. Awareness: what do they already know?
5. Desire/anxiety: what do they want or fear?
6. One promise: what main outcome can we honestly claim?
7. Mechanism: why can this product deliver differently/better?
8. Proof: what evidence supports the claim?
9. Objections: what would stop a reasonable buyer?
10. CTA: what is the smallest honest next step?
11. Ethics/trust: are we using real persuasion or pressure cosplay?

## Page-level playbooks

### Homepage hero

Must include audience/problem signal, one simple promise, mechanism/proof cue, primary CTA, and optionally a secondary CTA for colder visitors.

Avoid: all-in-one sludge, puns before clarity, unsupported superlatives, and hero copy that could fit 100 competitors.

### Landing page

Structure: lead matched to awareness → promise/mechanism → outcomes → proof stack → objections → offer/CTA → FAQ/trust close.

### Pricing page

Reduce risk and make plan choice safe. Include plan-fit labels, value metric, hidden-cost/cancellation clarity, guarantee/trial/support reassurance, and FAQ.

### Comparison page

Help the user choose confidently using honest criteria, tradeoffs, switching-risk handling, buyer-specific proof, and respectful competitor framing.

### Testimonials/proof

Strong proof includes who said it, what changed, how much/how fast/under what conditions, and why that person/company resembles the target reader.

### CTA/form copy

CTA should tell the user what happens next. Forms should explain why information is needed and what commitment is implied.

## Trust boundaries

- No fake scarcity.
- No fake social proof.
- No invented authority.
- No hidden commitment.
- No “AI-powered” as a magic substitute for mechanism.
- No “unlock your potential” filler.

## Anti-patterns

| Anti-pattern | Why it fails | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Vague benefit soup | No concrete belief | Specific outcome + proof |
| Feature dumping | No user job | Translate feature → outcome → proof |
| Over-clever headline | Attention without comprehension | Clear promise first |
| Fake urgency | Trust damage | Use real constraint or remove |
| Empty logo wall | Weak relevance | Add peer/context/results |
| AI-powered | Mechanism placeholder | Explain what AI does and why it matters |
| Pain-only copy | Feels manipulative | Pair pain with desired future and agency |
| Pretty but unsupported | Design without argument | Add proof/mechanism/CTA clarity |

## Skill instruction summary

The skill should enforce: diagnose surface/audience/awareness/job → extract desire/anxiety/offer/proof → choose one promise and mechanism → draft/critique proof/objection/CTA path → run anti-pattern and ethics checks → output copy, critique, or acceptance criteria.
